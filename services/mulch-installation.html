Frequently Asked Questions

How often should mulch be replaced?
Most hardwood mulch should be refreshed annually to maintain appearance and proper depth. Cedar mulch typically lasts longer due to its durability and natural resistance to decay.
Do you remove old mulch before installing new mulch?
In most cases, full removal isn't necessary. If the existing mulch layer is still in good condition, we properly prepare the bed and install fresh mulch at the correct depth. If there is excessive buildup or deterioration, we'll make a recommendation during your estimate.
How much mulch will I need?
That depends on your current coverage and your long-term goals. Some homeowners prefer a lighter refresh for appearance, while others want deeper coverage for stronger weed suppression and moisture retention. During your on-site assessment, we measure your beds and recommend the proper depth based on your needs.
Do you edge beds before installing mulch?
Yes. We trim a defined dirt edge around the bed to help prevent grass and weeds from creeping in and to create a clean separation from the lawn. We also offer professional metal edging installation for homeowners who want a more permanent, structured border.
Do you handle the material sourcing?
Yes. We coordinate material selection and sourcing so your installation is handled from start to finish without you needing to arrange delivery separately.
Do you install landscape fabric under the mulch?
We recommend against it, but we can install it if you request it. We could sell it as an add-on, but we'd rather be honest about what actually works for your landscape. Mulch is meant to break down into the soil over time and improve it. Fabric blocks that. Weeds still come through anyway because decomposed mulch builds up on top of the fabric and they root right into it. Now you've got a useless layer trapped underneath that's blocking nutrients and water from your soil, and if you ever want it out, you have to remove all the mulch just to get to it. Proper mulch depth handles weed suppression on its own.
